How to Calculate Your TDEE: A Complete Guide to Total Daily Energy Expenditure

📅 Feb 21, 2025 · 🏷️ TDEE, Calories, Nutrition · 8 min read

Whether you're trying to lose weight, build muscle, or simply maintain your current physique, there's one number you absolutely need to know: your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). TDEE represents the total number of calories your body burns in a day, and it's the foundation of any effective nutrition plan.

What Is TDEE?

TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It's the total number of calories you burn every day, including everything from breathing and digesting food to exercising and walking to your car.

Your TDEE is made up of four components:

Component% of TDEEDescription
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate)60-70%Calories burned at complete rest — just to keep you alive (breathing, heart beating, brain functioning)
TEF (Thermic Effect of Food)8-15%Calories burned digesting and processing food
EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)5-10%Calories burned during intentional exercise
NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis)15-30%Calories burned through daily movement — walking, fidgeting, standing, household chores

The surprising truth? Exercise accounts for only 5-10% of your total calorie burn. Your BMR and NEAT are far more significant, which is why you can't "out-exercise" a bad diet.

How to Calculate Your BMR

The first step in calculating TDEE is finding your BMR. The two most commonly used formulas are:

Mifflin-St Jeor Equation (Most Accurate)

Men: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age) + 5
Women: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) - (5 × age) - 161

Harris-Benedict Equation (Classic)

Men: BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 × weight in kg) + (4.799 × height in cm) - (5.677 × age)
Women: BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 × weight in kg) + (3.098 × height in cm) - (4.330 × age)

Research has shown that the Mifflin-St Jeor equation is the most accurate for most people, which is why our TDEE calculator uses it as the default.

Example Calculation

Let's calculate BMR for a 30-year-old male, 175 cm tall, weighing 75 kg:

  1. BMR = (10 × 75) + (6.25 × 175) - (5 × 30) + 5
  2. BMR = 750 + 1,093.75 - 150 + 5
  3. BMR = 1,698.75 calories/day

This means his body burns approximately 1,699 calories per day just to stay alive — before any physical activity.

From BMR to TDEE: Activity Multipliers

To get your TDEE, multiply your BMR by an activity factor:

Activity LevelMultiplierDescription
Sedentary1.2Desk job, little to no exercise
Lightly Active1.375Light exercise 1-3 days/week
Moderately Active1.55Moderate exercise 3-5 days/week
Very Active1.725Hard exercise 6-7 days/week
Extremely Active1.9Very hard exercise, physical job, or training twice a day

Continuing our example: If our 30-year-old male exercises 3 times a week (moderately active):

TDEE = 1,699 × 1.55 = 2,633 calories/day

How to Use TDEE for Your Goals

Goal 1: Lose Weight (Calorie Deficit)

To lose weight, eat fewer calories than your TDEE. A moderate deficit of 300-500 calories per day is ideal for sustainable fat loss — about 0.25-0.5 kg (0.5-1 lb) per week.

  • Moderate deficit: TDEE - 300 to 500 calories
  • Aggressive deficit: TDEE - 500 to 750 calories (harder to sustain)
  • Never go below: 1,200 (women) / 1,500 (men) without medical supervision

For our example: 2,633 - 400 = ~2,230 calories/day for steady weight loss.

Goal 2: Maintain Weight

Eat at your TDEE. Simple as that. This is useful when you're happy with your current weight and want to maintain it while optimizing body composition.

Goal 3: Build Muscle (Calorie Surplus)

To gain muscle, eat more than your TDEE combined with resistance training. A moderate surplus ensures most gains are lean muscle rather than fat.

  • Lean bulk: TDEE + 200 to 300 calories
  • Standard bulk: TDEE + 300 to 500 calories

For our example: 2,633 + 250 = ~2,880 calories/day for lean muscle gain.

Common Mistakes When Using TDEE

Mistake 1: Overestimating Activity Level

This is by far the most common mistake. Most people with desk jobs who exercise 3-4 times per week are "Lightly Active" or at most "Moderately Active." If in doubt, round down.

Mistake 2: Treating TDEE as an Exact Number

TDEE calculations are estimates, not gospel. Your actual TDEE can vary by 10-15% based on genetics, hormones, sleep, stress, and other factors. Use it as a starting point, then adjust based on real-world results.

Mistake 3: Not Adjusting Over Time

As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases (smaller body = fewer calories needed). Recalculate every 4-6 weeks or when you hit a plateau.

Mistake 4: Ignoring NEAT

NEAT (non-exercise activity) can vary by up to 2,000 calories between individuals. If you're trying to lose weight, increasing your daily steps (aim for 8,000-10,000) can be more effective than adding another gym session.

Mistake 5: Eating Back Exercise Calories

Fitness trackers notoriously overestimate exercise calories by 20-90%. If your TDEE already accounts for your activity level, don't add exercise calories on top.

How to Track and Verify Your TDEE

The most accurate way to determine your actual TDEE is through real-world tracking:

  1. Calculate your estimated TDEE using our TDEE calculator
  2. Track your calories accurately for 2-3 weeks (use a food scale for best results)
  3. Weigh yourself daily at the same time (morning, after bathroom) and take weekly averages
  4. Compare: If your weight is stable, your average intake ≈ your real TDEE. If you're losing ~0.5 kg/week, your real TDEE is about 500 calories higher than your intake.

This empirical approach removes the guesswork from TDEE formulas and gives you a number that's personalized to your unique metabolism.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Should I eat the same amount every day?

Not necessarily. Many people prefer calorie cycling — eating more on training days and less on rest days. As long as your weekly average aligns with your goal, the daily distribution is flexible.

Q: Does TDEE change with age?

Yes. TDEE typically decreases by about 1-2% per decade after age 20, primarily due to loss of muscle mass. Strength training can slow this decline significantly.

Q: Is TDEE the same as "metabolism"?

Essentially, yes. When people say they have a "fast" or "slow" metabolism, they're really talking about a higher or lower TDEE. The difference between a "fast" and "slow" metabolism is typically only 200-300 calories — much less dramatic than most people assume.

Q: How does muscle mass affect TDEE?

Muscle is more metabolically active than fat. Each pound of muscle burns approximately 6-7 calories per day at rest, compared to 2-3 for fat. While this difference seems small, it adds up — 10 lbs of extra muscle burns an additional 40-50 calories per day.

Summary

Understanding your TDEE is the single most important step in taking control of your nutrition. Here's what to remember:

  • TDEE = BMR × Activity Multiplier
  • Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation for the most accurate BMR estimate
  • Don't overestimate your activity level — when in doubt, round down
  • TDEE is an estimate — track and adjust based on real results
  • For weight loss: eat 300-500 calories below your TDEE
  • For muscle gain: eat 200-300 calories above your TDEE
  • Recalculate every 4-6 weeks as your body changes
